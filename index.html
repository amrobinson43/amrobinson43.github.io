Bio

A brief overview of my academic background, research interests, and current work.

I am an advanced PhD candidate in Geography and Environment at UNC Chapel Hill. My research examines how land-surface characteristics, atmospheric conditions, and urban form interact to shape hyper-local heat stress patterns. I use citizen science observations, geospatial data, and machine learning to model Wet Bulb Globe Temperature and related environmental variables at fine spatial scales.

My current work sits at the intersection of urban climatology, environmental modeling, and applied geographic analysis. Across my dissertation and related projects, I have focused on quantifying how urban form can amplify or reduce heat stress, and developing methods that can support climate risk communication and local adaptation planning.

In addition to research, I am interested in reproducible scientific workflows and the use of observational and modeling approaches to answer practical environmental questions.

WBGT Prediction Tool

An interactive tool for estimating neighborhood-scale Wet Bulb Globe Temperature across Carrboro and Chapel Hill from broad-scale ambient conditions.

Interactive Research Application

Downscaled WBGT prediction for Carrboro and Chapel Hill

This web tool predicts Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) at 100 m spatial resolution across Carrboro and Chapel Hill using ambient weather conditions such as the broader-scale reading you might see on your phone. It was developed from observations collected during the 2025 heat season citizen science campaign operated by the Southeast Regional Climate Center (SERCC).

The goal of the tool is to translate broader atmospheric conditions into neighborhood-scale heat stress estimates, helping users better understand how WBGT can vary across space within the urban environment. Rather than representing a single station value, the application provides a downscaled estimate of how heat stress may differ from one part of town to another.

The model performs best when input conditions resemble one of the 21 observation days used to train the application. It is also best suited for afternoon prediction, especially around 2:15 PM, when the observational framework and model design are most closely aligned with the underlying data.

Best use case: estimate likely afternoon WBGT patterns across Carrboro and Chapel Hill under conditions similar to those observed during the 2025 SERCC summer campaign.

Best prediction time
